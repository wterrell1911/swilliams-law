## Types of Car Accidents We Handle

Car accidents take many forms, and the circumstances of a collision often determine who is liable and how much compensation a victim may be entitled to recover. S. Williams Law Firm handles a wide range of vehicle crash cases, including:

### Rear-End Collisions

Rear-end accidents are among the most common types of traffic collisions. They frequently occur in stop-and-go traffic, at red lights, and in highway merging zones. While many people assume rear-end collisions are minor, they can cause serious neck, back, and spinal cord injuries, especially at higher speeds.

### Head-On Collisions

Head-on crashes occur when two vehicles traveling in opposite directions collide front-to-front. These automobile injury cases often result in catastrophic or fatal injuries due to the combined force of impact. Wrong-way drivers, distracted motorists, and impaired drivers are common causes.

### Side-Impact (T-Bone) Crashes

T-bone collisions typically happen at intersections when one driver fails to yield the right of way or runs a red light. Because the side of a vehicle provides less structural protection than the front or rear, occupants on the impact side are particularly vulnerable to severe injuries.

### Multi-Vehicle Pileups

Chain-reaction accidents involving three or more vehicles present complex liability questions. These car wrecks are common on Georgia interstates such as I-285 and I-75 and on Mississippi highways including I-55 and I-20. Determining fault may require extensive investigation and accident reconstruction.

### Single-Vehicle Accidents

Not all car accidents involve multiple vehicles. Defective road conditions, poor signage, vehicle defects, or debris in the roadway can cause a driver to lose control. In these cases, liability may rest with a government entity, a vehicle manufacturer, or another third party.

### Hit-and-Run Accidents

When a negligent driver flees the scene, victims may feel helpless. However, Georgia and Mississippi law provide avenues for recovery through uninsured motorist coverage and other legal mechanisms. S. Williams Law Firm works to identify hit-and-run drivers and pursue all available sources of compensation.

## Common Car Accident Injuries

The injuries sustained in a vehicle crash can range from mild to life-threatening. Some of the most common injuries our clients suffer include:

- **Whiplash and soft tissue injuries** -- Neck strain caused by the sudden back-and-forth motion of the head during impact. Symptoms may not appear for hours or days after the accident.
- **Traumatic brain injuries (TBI)** -- Concussions and more severe brain injuries can result from striking the head against the steering wheel, window, or headrest. Even mild TBIs can have lasting cognitive effects.
- **Spinal cord injuries and paralysis** -- Damage to the spinal cord can result in partial or complete paralysis, requiring lifelong medical care and adaptive equipment.
- **Broken bones and fractures** -- Arms, legs, ribs, and facial bones are frequently fractured in car accidents. Complex fractures may require surgery and extended rehabilitation.
- **Internal organ damage** -- The force of a collision can cause internal bleeding and organ damage that may not be immediately apparent without medical imaging.
- **Burns** -- Vehicle fires and contact with hot surfaces or fluids can cause serious burn injuries requiring skin grafts and ongoing treatment.
- **Psychological injuries** -- Post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD), anxiety, depression, and driving phobias are common after serious auto collisions and are recognized as compensable injuries.

Regardless of the type of injury you have sustained, it is critical to seek medical attention immediately after a car accident. Prompt medical documentation not only protects your health but also strengthens your legal claim.

### Statute of Limitations

One of the most important legal deadlines in any car accident case is the statute of limitations -- the time period within which you must file a lawsuit or forfeit your right to seek compensation.

- **Georgia:** Under O.C.G.A. 9-3-33, the statute of limitations for personal injury claims arising from a car accident is **two (2) years** from the date of the accident. For property damage claims, the deadline is four (4) years under O.C.G.A. 9-3-30.
- **Mississippi:** Under Miss. Code Ann. 15-1-49, the statute of limitations for personal injury claims is **three (3) years** from the date of the accident. Property damage claims also carry a three-year deadline.

### Comparative Fault Rules

Both Georgia and Mississippi follow versions of comparative fault, but the rules differ in important ways.

{/* ATTORNEY REVIEW */}
- **Georgia** follows a **modified comparative negligence** standard (O.C.G.A. 51-11-7). You may recover damages as long as you are less than 50 percent at fault for the accident. Your compensation is reduced by your percentage of fault. If you are 50 percent or more at fault, you are barred from recovering any damages.
- **Mississippi** follows a **pure comparative negligence** standard. Under Mississippi law, you may recover damages even if you are 99 percent at fault, though your award is reduced by your degree of fault.

Understanding how comparative fault applies to your case is critical because insurance companies routinely attempt to shift blame onto injured victims to reduce or eliminate payouts.

### Insurance Requirements

{/* ATTORNEY REVIEW */}
Georgia requires all drivers to carry minimum liability insurance of $25,000 per person and $50,000 per accident for bodily injury, plus $25,000 for property damage. Mississippi requires minimum liability coverage of $25,000 per person and $50,000 per accident for bodily injury, plus $25,000 for property damage. Neither state operates under a no-fault insurance system, meaning injured parties may file claims directly against the at-fault driver's insurance.

## What to Do After a Car Accident

The steps you take in the hours and days following a traffic collision can significantly affect the outcome of your case. S. Williams Law Firm recommends the following:

1. **Call 911.** Report the accident to law enforcement. A police report creates an official record of the collision and the responding officer's observations.
2. **Seek immediate medical attention.** Even if you feel fine, some injuries -- particularly whiplash, concussions, and internal injuries -- have delayed symptoms. A prompt medical evaluation documents your injuries and connects them to the accident.
3. **Document the scene.** If you are physically able, take photographs of the vehicles, the roadway, traffic signals, skid marks, debris, and any visible injuries. Collect the names and contact information of witnesses.
4. **Exchange information.** Obtain the other driver's name, phone number, insurance information, driver's license number, and license plate number.
5. **Do not admit fault.** Avoid making statements like "I'm sorry" or "It was my fault." These statements can be used against you later.
6. **Notify your insurance company.** Report the accident to your own insurer, but do not provide a recorded statement or accept a settlement offer without first consulting an attorney.
7. **Contact S. Williams Law Firm.** Before speaking with the at-fault driver's insurance company, contact Attorney Williams for a free case evaluation. Early legal representation can prevent costly mistakes.

## How Car Accident Compensation Is Calculated

The value of a car accident claim depends on many factors. Compensation in Georgia and Mississippi auto collision cases generally falls into three categories:

### Economic Damages

Economic damages compensate you for the financial losses directly caused by the accident. These include:

- Past and future medical expenses (emergency care, surgery, rehabilitation, prescription medications, medical devices)
- Lost wages and lost earning capacity
- Property damage (vehicle repair or replacement)
- Out-of-pocket expenses (transportation to medical appointments, home modifications)

### Non-Economic Damages

Non-economic damages address the intangible harm you have suffered, including:

- Physical pain and suffering
- Mental anguish and emotional distress
- Loss of enjoyment of life
- Loss of consortium (the impact on your relationship with your spouse)
- Scarring and disfigurement

### Punitive Damages

{/* ATTORNEY REVIEW */}
In cases involving egregious conduct -- such as drunk driving or extreme recklessness -- Georgia and Mississippi courts may award punitive damages. In Georgia, punitive damages are generally capped at $250,000 (with exceptions for cases involving specific intent or intoxication under O.C.G.A. 51-12-5.1). Mississippi does not impose a statutory cap on punitive damages in most personal injury cases, though the Mississippi Supreme Court has addressed proportionality in its case law.

## Insurance Company Tactics to Watch For

Insurance companies are businesses driven by profit. Their adjusters and attorneys are trained to minimize payouts by any means necessary. Common tactics include:

- **Quick lowball settlement offers.** Insurers often contact accident victims within days to offer a fast settlement far below the true value of the claim, hoping victims will accept before understanding the full extent of their injuries.
- **Requesting recorded statements.** Adjusters ask pointed questions designed to elicit responses that can be used to reduce or deny your claim.
- **Disputing medical treatment.** Insurance companies may argue that your injuries are pre-existing, unrelated to the accident, or that you received unnecessary treatment.
- **Surveillance.** Insurers sometimes hire investigators to monitor claimants on social media or in person, looking for evidence that contradicts injury claims.
- **Delay tactics.** By dragging out the claims process, insurers pressure victims facing financial hardship to accept less than they deserve.
- **Blaming the victim.** Under Georgia's modified comparative fault rules, shifting even a portion of blame to the victim can dramatically reduce or eliminate the insurer's obligation to pay.
