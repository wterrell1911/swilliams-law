## Federal Trucking Regulations (FMCSA)

The commercial trucking industry is heavily regulated at the federal level by the Federal Motor Carrier Safety Administration (FMCSA). These regulations exist because of the extraordinary danger that commercial vehicles pose to the public. When trucking companies and their drivers violate these regulations, they may be held liable for the injuries and deaths that result.

### Hours of Service (HOS) Rules

{/* ATTORNEY REVIEW */}
FMCSA Hours of Service regulations (49 CFR Part 395) limit how long commercial truck drivers may operate their vehicles before taking mandatory rest breaks. Key provisions include:

- **11-Hour Driving Limit:** A driver may drive a maximum of 11 hours after 10 consecutive hours off duty.
- **14-Hour On-Duty Limit:** A driver may not drive beyond the 14th consecutive hour after coming on duty, regardless of breaks taken during that period.
- **30-Minute Rest Break:** Drivers must take a 30-minute break after 8 cumulative hours of driving.
- **60/70-Hour Weekly Limit:** Drivers may not drive after 60 hours on duty in 7 consecutive days or 70 hours in 8 consecutive days.

Violations of HOS rules are a leading cause of fatigued driving among commercial truck operators. Electronic logging devices (ELDs) are now required on most commercial vehicles to record driving time, though some carriers still attempt to falsify records.

### Driver Qualification Standards

{/* ATTORNEY REVIEW */}
Under 49 CFR Part 391, trucking companies must ensure their drivers meet minimum qualification standards, including holding a valid commercial driver's license (CDL), passing medical examinations, undergoing drug and alcohol testing, and satisfying minimum age requirements (21 for interstate driving). Negligent hiring occurs when a carrier employs a driver with a disqualifying safety record, a history of substance abuse, or inadequate training.

### Vehicle Maintenance and Inspection Requirements

{/* ATTORNEY REVIEW */}
FMCSA regulations (49 CFR Parts 393 and 396) require trucking companies to maintain their vehicles in safe operating condition and to conduct regular inspections. Drivers must perform pre-trip and post-trip inspections, and carriers must maintain detailed maintenance records. Brake failures, tire blowouts, and lighting deficiencies caused by inadequate maintenance are frequent factors in semi-truck collisions.

### Cargo Loading and Securement

{/* ATTORNEY REVIEW */}
Under 49 CFR Part 393, cargo must be properly loaded, distributed, and secured to prevent shifting during transit. Improperly loaded cargo can cause a truck to become unstable, jackknife, or roll over. Cargo that falls from a truck onto the roadway creates deadly hazards for other motorists.

## Types of Truck Accidents

The mechanics of big rig accidents differ significantly from typical passenger vehicle collisions. Understanding the type of accident is essential for establishing liability and building a strong case.

### Jackknife Accidents

A jackknife accident occurs when a truck's trailer swings outward at an angle from the cab, folding like a closing pocketknife. Jackknifing is often caused by sudden braking, slippery road conditions, or equipment failure. A jackknifed truck can sweep across multiple lanes of traffic, striking numerous vehicles.

### Rollover Accidents

Tractor-trailers have a high center of gravity, making them vulnerable to rollovers, especially when taking curves at excessive speed, when cargo shifts, or in high-wind conditions. Rollover accidents frequently result in catastrophic injuries to the occupants of nearby vehicles.

### Underride Accidents

Underride crashes occur when a smaller vehicle slides beneath the trailer of a truck, often shearing off the top of the passenger vehicle. Rear underrides and side underrides are among the most lethal types of truck accidents. Federal regulations require rear underride guards, but enforcement is inconsistent and side underride guards are not yet universally mandated.

### Blind Spot (No-Zone) Accidents

Commercial trucks have large blind spots -- known as "no-zones" -- on all four sides. When truck drivers fail to check their mirrors or use proper lane-change techniques, they can merge into or sideswipe vehicles in their blind spots.

### Wide Turn Accidents

Tractor-trailers require a wide turning radius. When making right turns, truck drivers may swing left first and then turn right, creating a gap that other vehicles may enter. Motorists caught in this gap can be crushed between the truck and the curb or other obstacles.

### Rear-End Collisions

Due to their enormous weight, commercial trucks require significantly longer stopping distances than passenger vehicles. When a truck driver is following too closely or is distracted, the consequences of a rear-end collision with a smaller vehicle can be devastating.

## Multiple Liable Parties in Truck Accident Cases

One of the factors that makes truck accident litigation more complex than typical car accident cases is the number of potentially liable parties. Unlike a standard auto collision where liability usually rests with one or two drivers, a commercial vehicle crash may involve multiple defendants:

- **The truck driver** -- The driver may be liable for negligent driving, speeding, distracted driving, impaired driving, or violations of Hours of Service regulations.
- **The trucking company (motor carrier)** -- Under the legal doctrine of respondeat superior, a trucking company may be vicariously liable for the negligent acts of its employees. The carrier may also be directly liable for negligent hiring, inadequate training, failure to maintain vehicles, or pressure on drivers to violate safety regulations.
- **The cargo loading company** -- If improperly loaded or secured cargo contributed to the accident, the company responsible for loading the trailer may share liability.
- **The truck or parts manufacturer** -- If a defective component -- such as faulty brakes, a defective tire, or a malfunctioning steering system -- caused or contributed to the accident, the manufacturer may be liable under product liability law.
- **Maintenance providers** -- Third-party mechanics and maintenance companies may be liable if negligent repairs or inspections contributed to the crash.
- **Government entities** -- If dangerous road conditions, inadequate signage, or defective road design contributed to the accident, a state or local government agency may bear some responsibility. Note that claims against government entities are subject to special notice requirements and shortened deadlines under Georgia and Mississippi law.

Identifying all liable parties is essential to maximizing the compensation available to truck accident victims. S. Williams Law Firm conducts thorough investigations to determine every party that contributed to the crash.

## Common Causes of Truck Accidents

Understanding the root cause of an 18-wheeler accident is critical for establishing liability. The most common causes include:

- **Driver fatigue** -- Despite Hours of Service regulations, fatigued driving remains one of the leading causes of commercial truck accidents. Carriers that pressure drivers to meet unrealistic delivery schedules bear significant responsibility.
- **Distracted driving** -- Texting, using GPS devices, eating, and other distractions are particularly dangerous for drivers operating 80,000-pound vehicles.
- **Impaired driving** -- Alcohol and drug use, including the misuse of stimulants to combat fatigue, impair judgment, reaction time, and vehicle control.
- **Speeding and aggressive driving** -- Driving too fast for conditions or driving aggressively dramatically increases stopping distances and reduces the driver's ability to react to hazards.
- **Inadequate training** -- Trucking companies that fail to properly train their drivers in defensive driving techniques, cargo securement, and vehicle operation put the public at risk.
- **Poor vehicle maintenance** -- Brake failures, tire blowouts, broken lights, and other mechanical deficiencies caused by deferred maintenance are preventable causes of deadly crashes.
- **Adverse weather** -- Rain, fog, ice, and wind require commercial truck drivers to adjust their driving behavior. Failure to reduce speed or increase following distance in poor conditions constitutes negligence.

## Serious Injuries in Truck Accidents

Due to the extreme forces involved in collisions with commercial vehicles, truck accident injuries tend to be far more severe than those in typical car accidents. Common injuries include:

- **Traumatic brain injuries (TBI)** -- Ranging from concussions to severe brain damage resulting in permanent cognitive, behavioral, and physical impairments.
- **Spinal cord injuries and paralysis** -- Complete or incomplete spinal cord damage that may result in paraplegia or quadriplegia, requiring lifelong medical care.
- **Crush injuries and amputations** -- The weight and force of a commercial truck can crush limbs and extremities, sometimes requiring surgical amputation.
- **Severe burns** -- Truck accidents involving fuel spills or hazardous material cargo can cause devastating burn injuries.
- **Multiple fractures** -- Complex fractures requiring multiple surgeries, hardware implantation, and extended rehabilitation.
- **Internal organ damage** -- Ruptured organs and internal bleeding that may require emergency surgery and prolonged hospitalization.
- **Wrongful death** -- Tragically, many truck accidents are fatal. S. Williams Law Firm also handles wrongful death claims arising from commercial vehicle crashes.

The medical costs associated with these injuries can reach hundreds of thousands or even millions of dollars over a victim's lifetime. Attorney Williams fights to ensure truck accident victims receive compensation that fully accounts for their current and future needs.

## Georgia and Mississippi Truck Accident Laws

{/* ATTORNEY REVIEW */}
### Statute of Limitations

The time limits for filing a truck accident lawsuit vary by state:

- **Georgia:** Personal injury claims must be filed within **two (2) years** of the accident date under O.C.G.A. 9-3-33. Wrongful death claims must also be filed within two years of the date of death under O.C.G.A. 9-3-33.
- **Mississippi:** Personal injury claims must be filed within **three (3) years** of the accident date under Miss. Code Ann. 15-1-49. Wrongful death claims carry a three-year statute of limitations under Miss. Code Ann. 15-1-36.

{/* ATTORNEY REVIEW */}
### Comparative Fault

Georgia's modified comparative fault system bars recovery if the plaintiff is 50 percent or more at fault. Mississippi's pure comparative fault system allows recovery regardless of the plaintiff's percentage of fault, reduced proportionally. In truck accident cases, the defendant trucking company will almost always attempt to assign partial blame to the injured motorist to reduce its exposure. Attorney Williams aggressively combats these blame-shifting tactics.

### Punitive Damages

{/* ATTORNEY REVIEW */}
When a trucking company's conduct is particularly egregious -- such as knowingly allowing a driver to violate Hours of Service rules, falsifying maintenance records, or employing a driver with a documented history of substance abuse -- Georgia and Mississippi courts may award punitive damages in addition to compensatory damages. In Georgia, punitive damages are generally capped at $250,000 unless specific exceptions apply under O.C.G.A. 51-12-5.1. Mississippi does not impose a statutory cap on punitive damages in most cases.

## Evidence Preservation in Truck Accident Cases

Evidence in truck accident cases can disappear quickly. Trucking companies have legal obligations to preserve certain records, but critical evidence may be lost, overwritten, or destroyed if immediate action is not taken. That is why it is essential to retain an attorney as soon as possible after an 18-wheeler accident.

S. Williams Law Firm takes immediate steps to preserve and obtain evidence, including:

- **Sending spoliation letters** -- Formal legal notices demanding that the trucking company, its insurer, and all relevant parties preserve all evidence related to the accident.
- **Obtaining electronic logging device (ELD) data** -- ELDs record driving hours, speed, and other critical data that can prove Hours of Service violations or driver fatigue.
- **Securing the truck's event data recorder (EDR or "black box")** -- The EDR captures data such as speed, braking, throttle position, and seatbelt use in the seconds before a collision.
- **Collecting driver qualification files** -- These files contain the driver's employment history, CDL status, medical certifications, drug and alcohol test results, and training records.
- **Reviewing maintenance and inspection records** -- These records can reveal whether the truck was in safe operating condition at the time of the crash.
- **Obtaining dispatch and communication records** -- Text messages, dispatch logs, and GPS data can show whether the driver was under pressure to meet unrealistic deadlines.
- **Gathering surveillance and dashcam footage** -- Video from traffic cameras, nearby businesses, and onboard cameras can provide critical visual evidence of how the accident occurred.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How are truck accident cases different from car accident cases?

Truck accident cases are significantly more complex than typical car accident claims. They involve federal regulations (FMCSA rules), multiple potentially liable parties (driver, carrier, loader, manufacturer), specialized evidence (ELD data, black box data, driver qualification files), and often much higher damages due to the severity of injuries. These cases require an attorney who understands both the legal and technical aspects of commercial trucking.

### Who is liable for a truck accident -- the driver or the trucking company?

Often, both the driver and the trucking company share liability. Under the legal doctrine of respondeat superior, an employer is generally liable for the negligent acts of its employees performed within the scope of employment. The trucking company may also be directly liable for negligent hiring, inadequate training, failure to maintain vehicles, or pressuring drivers to violate safety regulations. In some cases, additional parties such as cargo loaders, parts manufacturers, and maintenance providers may also be liable.

### How long do I have to file a truck accident lawsuit?

{/* ATTORNEY REVIEW */}
In Georgia, the statute of limitations for personal injury claims is two (2) years from the date of the accident. In Mississippi, it is three (3) years. However, evidence in truck accident cases can be lost or destroyed quickly, so it is critical to retain an attorney as soon as possible -- ideally within days of the accident -- to preserve essential evidence.

### What compensation can I receive for a truck accident?

Truck accident victims may be entitled to compensation for medical expenses (past and future), lost wages and reduced earning capacity, pain and suffering, emotional distress, loss of enjoyment of life, property damage, and in some cases, punitive damages. Because truck accident injuries tend to be severe, the total value of these claims is often substantially higher than in typical car accident cases.

### Can a trucking company destroy evidence after an accident?

Trucking companies are legally obligated to preserve certain records, including driver logs, maintenance records, and qualification files. However, some evidence -- such as dashcam footage and ELD data -- may be routinely overwritten if a preservation demand is not issued promptly. S. Williams Law Firm sends spoliation letters immediately upon being retained to ensure that all relevant evidence is preserved. Destruction of evidence can result in adverse legal consequences for the trucking company.
