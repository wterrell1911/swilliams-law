## Understanding Georgia's Wrongful Death Law

Georgia has a specific statutory framework governing wrongful death claims, found in Title 51, Chapter 4 of the Official Code of Georgia Annotated. {/* ATTORNEY REVIEW: O.C.G.A. § 51-4-1 et seq. — Verify current wrongful death statute provisions, including any recent amendments */} Understanding this framework is essential because Georgia's wrongful death laws differ from those of many other states in important ways.

**What Constitutes Wrongful Death.** Under Georgia law, a wrongful death occurs when a person's death is caused by a "criminal or negligent act, or by the negligent failure to act." {/* ATTORNEY REVIEW: O.C.G.A. § 51-4-1 — Confirm statutory language defining wrongful death */} In other words, if the deceased person would have had a valid personal injury claim had they survived, their family may bring a wrongful death action instead.

**Who Can File a Wrongful Death Claim.** Georgia law establishes a clear hierarchy for who has standing to file a wrongful death lawsuit. {/* ATTORNEY REVIEW: O.C.G.A. § 51-4-2 — Verify standing requirements and order of priority for filing */}

- The **surviving spouse** has the first right to file and will share any recovery equally with the surviving children, though the spouse must receive at least one-third of the total recovery
- If there is **no surviving spouse**, the children of the deceased may file the claim
- If there are **no surviving children**, the parents of the deceased may bring the action
- If none of the above exist, the **personal representative or administrator of the estate** may file on behalf of the next of kin

This hierarchy is strictly enforced by Georgia courts, and filing by the wrong party can result in dismissal of the case. S. Williams Law Firm carefully evaluates your family's situation to ensure the claim is brought by the proper party from the outset.

**Statute of Limitations.** A wrongful death lawsuit in Georgia must generally be filed within two years of the date of death. {/* ATTORNEY REVIEW: O.C.G.A. § 9-3-33 — Verify statute of limitations for wrongful death and any tolling exceptions */} In certain limited circumstances, the deadline may be tolled or extended, but you should never rely on exceptions. Evidence deteriorates, witnesses become harder to locate, and the responsible parties may take steps to shield their assets. Contacting an attorney as soon as you are ready is one of the most important things you can do to protect your family's rights.

## Damages in a Georgia Wrongful Death Case

Georgia's approach to wrongful death damages is unique compared to many other states. The law allows the surviving family to recover the "full value of the life of the decedent." {/* ATTORNEY REVIEW: O.C.G.A. § 51-4-1 — Verify "full value of life" standard and how Georgia courts have interpreted this */} This includes both the economic and the intangible value of the deceased person's life.

**Economic Value.** This encompasses the financial contributions the deceased would have made over the course of their expected lifetime, including:

- Lost income and earning capacity
- Lost employment benefits, including retirement contributions and health insurance
- The value of household services the deceased provided
- Medical and funeral expenses incurred as a result of the fatal injury

**Intangible Value.** Georgia law also recognizes that a person's life has value beyond what can be calculated in dollars. The intangible value of a life includes:

- Loss of the deceased's companionship, love, and affection
- Loss of guidance and counsel, particularly significant when the deceased was a parent to minor children
- Loss of the comfort and society of the deceased
- The pain and suffering the deceased experienced before death, if applicable

In addition to the wrongful death claim itself, the estate of the deceased may bring a separate survival action to recover damages for the pain and suffering, medical expenses, and other losses the deceased experienced between the time of injury and death.
