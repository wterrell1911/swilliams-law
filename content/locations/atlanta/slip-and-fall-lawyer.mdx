## Understanding Georgia Premises Liability Law

Slip and fall cases in Georgia are governed by premises liability law, which is a subset of personal injury law focused on injuries that occur on someone else's property. The foundation of any premises liability claim is the concept of the property owner's duty of care to people who enter their property.

Georgia law recognizes different categories of visitors, each of which is owed a different level of care: {/* ATTORNEY REVIEW: O.C.G.A. § 51-3-1 et seq. — Verify current premises liability statute provisions and visitor classifications */}

**Invitees.** These are people who enter a property for a purpose connected to the owner's business or for a purpose for which the property is held open to the public. Customers in a retail store, diners in a restaurant, tenants in an apartment building, and visitors in a hotel are all examples of invitees. Property owners owe invitees the highest duty of care — they must exercise ordinary care to keep the premises safe and must inspect the property for hidden dangers.

**Licensees.** These are people who enter a property for their own purposes with the owner's permission but not for the owner's direct benefit. Social guests at a private home are a common example. Property owners must refrain from willfully or wantonly injuring licensees and must warn them of known dangers that are not obvious.

**Trespassers.** Property owners generally owe the least duty to trespassers, though there are important exceptions, particularly involving children under the attractive nuisance doctrine. {/* ATTORNEY REVIEW: Verify Georgia's attractive nuisance doctrine application and any relevant case law updates */}

Most slip and fall cases in Atlanta involve invitees — people who were lawfully present at a business, commercial property, or public space when they were injured.

## Proving Negligence in an Atlanta Slip and Fall Case

To succeed in a slip and fall claim in Georgia, you must establish several key elements:

1. **The property owner owed you a duty of care.** If you were an invitee on the property, the owner had a duty to exercise ordinary care in maintaining the premises in a safe condition.

2. **A hazardous condition existed on the property.** This could be a wet floor, a loose railing, a broken step, an icy walkway, a pothole, torn carpeting, inadequate lighting, or any other condition that created an unreasonable risk of injury.

3. **The property owner knew or should have known about the hazard.** This is often the most contested element in a slip and fall case. Under Georgia law, you must show that the owner had actual knowledge of the hazardous condition, or that the condition existed for a sufficient length of time that the owner should have discovered it through the exercise of ordinary care. {/* ATTORNEY REVIEW: O.C.G.A. § 51-3-1 — Verify knowledge requirement and "should have known" standard under current Georgia case law */}

4. **The property owner failed to correct the hazard or warn you about it.** Even if a property owner knows about a danger, they can fulfill their duty by either fixing the problem or providing adequate warning (such as a wet floor sign). Failing to do either constitutes negligence.

5. **The hazardous condition caused your injury.** You must demonstrate that the specific condition on the property directly caused your fall and your resulting injuries.

Building a strong slip and fall case requires acting quickly to preserve evidence. Surveillance footage is often overwritten within days or weeks. Witnesses' memories fade. ## Common Slip and Fall Hazards in Atlanta

Atlanta's unique combination of commercial density, aging infrastructure, rapid development, and Southern weather patterns creates a wide range of slip and fall hazards throughout the metro area.

**Wet and Slippery Floors.** This is the most common cause of slip and fall injuries in commercial properties. Spilled liquids in grocery stores and restaurants, recently mopped floors without adequate warning signs, water tracked in from Atlanta's frequent rainstorms, and leaking roofs or plumbing all create dangerously slippery surfaces. Large retail centers and malls in Buckhead, Lenox Square, Phipps Plaza, and throughout the metro area see a significant number of these incidents.

**Uneven Surfaces and Broken Pavement.** Atlanta's aging sidewalks and parking lots, combined with the root systems of the city's abundant trees, create uneven walking surfaces that cause serious trip and fall injuries. Cracked and buckling sidewalks in Midtown and Downtown, potholed parking lots in commercial shopping centers, and deteriorating walkways in apartment complexes throughout Fulton and DeKalb Counties are common culprits.

**Inadequate Lighting.** Poorly lit parking garages, stairwells, walkways, and building entrances create conditions where visitors cannot see hazards in their path. Multi-story parking structures in Buckhead, Midtown, and near Hartsfield-Jackson Atlanta International Airport are frequent locations for falls caused by insufficient lighting.

**Stairway and Escalator Hazards.** Broken handrails, loose carpeting on stairs, worn stair treads, wet steps, and malfunctioning escalators in commercial buildings, MARTA stations, apartment complexes, and office towers throughout Atlanta cause painful and often debilitating injuries.

**Construction Debris and Defects.** With Atlanta's construction boom, particularly along the BeltLine and in rapidly developing neighborhoods, construction zones present hazards to pedestrians and neighboring property visitors. Uneven surfaces, exposed rebar, unsecured building materials, and inadequate barriers around construction sites are all potential causes of serious falls.

**Weather-Related Hazards.** Property owners in Atlanta have an obligation to address weather-related hazards within a reasonable time. This includes cleaning up standing water after summer thunderstorms, salting or sanding icy walkways during winter weather events, and maintaining drainage systems to prevent water accumulation on walkways and in parking areas.

## Georgia's Comparative Negligence in Slip and Fall Cases

Property owners and their insurance companies will almost always argue that you were partially responsible for your fall — that you were not watching where you were going, that you were wearing inappropriate footwear, or that you should have noticed the hazard and avoided it. Under Georgia's modified comparative negligence rule, your compensation may be reduced by your percentage of fault, but you can still recover as long as you were less than 50 percent responsible for the accident. {/* ATTORNEY REVIEW: O.C.G.A. § 51-12-33 — Verify comparative negligence application in premises liability cases */}
