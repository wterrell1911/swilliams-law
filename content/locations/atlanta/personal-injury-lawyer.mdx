## Understanding Georgia Personal Injury Law

Navigating Georgia's personal injury laws without experienced legal counsel can put your case at serious risk. There are critical deadlines and legal principles that directly affect your ability to recover compensation.

**Statute of Limitations.** Under Georgia law, you generally have two years from the date of your injury to file a personal injury lawsuit in civil court. {/* ATTORNEY REVIEW: O.C.G.A. § 9-3-33 — Verify current statute of limitations provisions and any recent amendments */} Missing this deadline almost always means losing your right to seek compensation entirely. While certain exceptions may apply — such as cases involving minors or injuries that were not immediately discovered — you should never assume you have more time than the standard two-year window.

**Modified Comparative Negligence.** Georgia follows a modified comparative negligence rule under O.C.G.A. Section 51-12-33. {/* ATTORNEY REVIEW: O.C.G.A. § 51-12-33 — Confirm current comparative negligence threshold and application */} This means you can still recover damages even if you were partially responsible for the accident, as long as your share of fault is less than 50 percent. However, your total compensation will be reduced by your percentage of fault. For example, if you are found to be 20 percent at fault and your damages total $100,000, your recovery would be reduced to $80,000. Insurance adjusters frequently try to shift blame onto injured victims to reduce what they owe. Having an experienced attorney who understands how to counter these tactics is essential.

**Types of Damages Available.** Georgia personal injury law allows you to seek both economic and non-economic damages. Economic damages include medical expenses — both past and future — lost wages, diminished earning capacity, and property damage. Non-economic damages cover pain and suffering, emotional distress, loss of enjoyment of life, and loss of consortium. In cases involving willful misconduct, fraud, or a pattern of reckless behavior, punitive damages may also be available to punish the wrongdoer and deter similar conduct. {/* ATTORNEY REVIEW: O.C.G.A. § 51-12-5.1 — Verify punitive damages cap and requirements */}
